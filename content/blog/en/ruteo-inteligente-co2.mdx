Every extra kilometer a truck drives is a triple waste: fuel burned, a delivery arriving later and CO₂ that didn't need to be emitted. Most fleets still plan routes with human judgment and a map — a method that works, but leaves 10% to 20% of efficiency on the table that an algorithm recovers effortlessly.

Smart routing doesn't replace the dispatcher's knowledge; it amplifies it. It takes the same constraints they juggle — delivery windows, vehicle capacity, customer priorities — and finds the combination that minimizes total distance, time and cost, in seconds instead of hours.

## Three levers that move together

What makes routing powerful is that a single good decision moves three indicators at once:

**Operating cost.** Fewer kilometers mean less fuel, less vehicle wear and more deliveries per shift with the same fleet. The [TMS](/plataforma/tms) computes the optimal assignment of orders to vehicles before the first truck leaves the yard.

**Service level.** Shorter, better-sequenced routes translate into delivery windows that are met. The customer receives when you said they would, and that shows up in repeat business.

**Carbon footprint.** Here's the figure many operations still don't measure: every kilometer avoided is CO₂ not emitted. When you optimize routes at scale, the emissions savings are substantial — and, increasingly, reportable.

## Sustainability as a metric, not a slogan

The big shift of recent years is that sustainability went from being a brand message to being a metric that customers, regulators and investors demand. "We're green" no longer cuts it; they ask for the number.

Smart routing produces that number naturally. By optimizing distance, the system knows exactly how many kilometers were saved and, with each vehicle type's emission factor, how much CO₂ was avoided. That figure stops being a year-end estimate and becomes a daily operational data point, ready for a sustainability report or a commercial proposal where environmental impact is a differentiator.

The [Control Tower](/plataforma/torre-control) consolidates that data alongside the rest of the operation, so environmental efficiency is measured with the same rigor as cost or compliance.

## Where it starts

You don't need to renew the fleet to start optimizing. The first step is to feed the [TMS](/plataforma/tms) your real orders, constraints and routes, and let it compare what you plan today against what the algorithm proposes. The gap between the two is usually the best justification for the project.

For [food and beverage](/industrias/alimentos) operations, where delivery volume is high and margins are tight, that recovered 10-20% of efficiency changes the month's result. And the CO₂ reduction that comes with it is, increasingly, an argument that opens doors.

Nearshoring put Mexico on the map: proximity to the world's largest market, trade agreements and skilled labor. Industrial buildings are filling up, parks are expanding and investments are announced every week. But there's one figure most expansion plans overlook, and it ends up deciding who captures the value: the cost of logistics.

In Mexico, moving goods costs between 13% and 14% of GDP. In OECD countries, that same process runs around 8%. That five-to-six point gap is not an accounting detail: it's the difference between an operation that leverages geographic proximity and one that wastes it on warehouses you can't see, inventory that doesn't reconcile and trucks that come back empty.

## Proximity is not enough

The promise of nearshoring is to deliver faster and cheaper than from Asia. But short distance only becomes an advantage if the internal operation is up to the task. A container that reaches port in days and then sits for a week in a bonded warehouse for lack of visibility loses all the time geography just gave it. The savings on the ocean leg evaporate in the yard, and the customer never feels the proximity that justified moving the supplier closer in the first place.

High logistics cost in Mexico doesn't come from freight rates, which are competitive. It comes from friction: manual relabeling, repeated physical counts, no traceability between supplier and warehouse, and route planning done by hand. Each of those frictions is a percentage point added to the 13-14%.

## Where the gap closes

The good news is that the gap is addressable with technology that already exists and doesn't require rebuilding the infrastructure.

**In the warehouse.** A [WMS](/plataforma/wms) that reconciles the supplier's format with yours eliminates hours of relabeling and counting. Inventory stops being an outdated snapshot and becomes live data, which is the basis for not over-buying or running short.

**In transport.** [Smart routing](/plataforma/tms) recovers 10% to 20% of fleet efficiency that manual planning leaves on the table. Fewer kilometers, more deliveries per shift, the same fleet.

**In visibility.** The [Control Tower](/plataforma/torre-control) unites what happens in the yard, the warehouse and the route in a single view. When you see the whole chain, bottlenecks stop being surprises and become decisions.

## Nearshoring that actually competes

The investment arriving in Mexico is chasing efficiency, not just proximity. The operations that close the gap between 13-14% and 8% will be the ones that turn location into real margin, and the ones that retain the customers nearshoring attracts.
